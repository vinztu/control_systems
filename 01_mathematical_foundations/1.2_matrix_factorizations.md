A matrix factorization rewrites a matrix $\mathbf{A}$ as a product of simpler, structured matrices (triangular, orthogonal, or diagonal) so that subsequent computations like solving linear systems, inverting matrices, or finding least-squares solutions become cheap and numerically well-behaved.

Rather than working with $\mathbf{A}$ directly, the pattern is always the same:

1. **Factor** $\mathbf{A}$ into structured pieces (done once, typically costs $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ for dense matrices, though special structure such as sparsity or symmetry can reduce this significantly).
2. **Solve or apply** the pieces to any right-hand side (repeated, costs $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$).

This *factor–solve* strategy is how almost every numerical algorithm in this course (from state estimation via the Kalman filter to model predictive control) solves linear systems under the hood.

---

## Condition Number

Before diving into specific factorizations, it is essential to understand the **condition number** of a matrix, since it appears repeatedly across all factorization methods as the key measure of numerical sensitivity.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Condition Number"}
For a nonsingular matrix $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, the condition number with respect to a matrix norm $\|\cdot\|$ is
$$
\kappa(\mathbf{A}) = \|\mathbf{A}\| \cdot \|\mathbf{A}^{-1}\|.
$$
For the Euclidean norm, this equals the ratio of the largest to the smallest singular value:
$$
\kappa(\mathbf{A}) = \frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_n}, \qquad \sigma_1 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_n > 0.
$$
:::

The condition number quantifies how much a small perturbation in the right-hand side $b$ can be amplified in the solution $x$ of $\mathbf{A}x = b$. Specifically, a relative perturbation of size $\epsilon$ in $b$ can produce a relative change of up to $\kappa(\mathbf{A}) \cdot \epsilon$ in $x$. A matrix with a large condition number is called **ill-conditioned** and computations involving it are sensitive to round-off and measurement errors. This matters throughout the course: covariance matrices in Kalman filtering, Hessians in MPC, and data matrices in system identification all need to be monitored for ill-conditioning.

## LU Factorization

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — LU Factorization"}
For $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ nonsingular, the LU factorization is
$$
\mathbf{P}\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{U},
$$
where $\mathbf{P}$ is a permutation matrix, $\mathbf{L}$ is unit lower triangular, and $\mathbf{U}$ is upper triangular. It always exists and can be computed in $\frac{2}{3}n^3$ flops via Gaussian elimination with partial pivoting.
:::

### What it is

Every nonsingular matrix $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ can be decomposed as

$$
\mathbf{P}\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{U},
$$

where

- $\mathbf{P}$ is a **permutation matrix** (reorders rows to improve numerical stability),
- $\mathbf{L}$ is **unit lower triangular** (ones on diagonal, zeros above),
- $\mathbf{U}$ is **upper triangular** (zeros below diagonal).

This factorization is computed by **Gaussian elimination with partial pivoting**.

### Forward and back substitution

Once $\mathbf{P}\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{U}$, solving $\mathbf{A}x = b$ proceeds in two steps. Starting from $\mathbf{A}x = b$, multiply both sides on the left by $\mathbf{P}$ to get $\mathbf{P}\mathbf{A}x = \mathbf{P}b$, and substitute the factorization to obtain $\mathbf{L}\mathbf{U}x = \mathbf{P}b$. Introducing the intermediate vector $z = \mathbf{U}x$ then splits the problem into two triangular systems:

$$
\mathbf{P}\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{U} \quad \Rightarrow \quad
\underbrace{\mathbf{L}z = \mathbf{P}b}_{\text{forward substitution}} \quad \text{then} \quad
\underbrace{\mathbf{U}x = z}_{\text{back substitution}}
$$

**Forward substitution** solves the lower triangular system $\mathbf{L}z = \mathbf{P}b$ by computing the unknowns from top to bottom. Since $\mathbf{L}$ is unit lower triangular, each unknown $z_i$ depends only on the already-computed values $z_1, \ldots, z_{i-1}$, so it can be obtained in one step without any further factorization.

**Back substitution** then solves the upper triangular system $\mathbf{U}x = z$ by computing unknowns from bottom to top. $x_n$ is found first directly, and each $x_i$ is obtained by substituting the already-known values $x_{i+1}, \ldots, x_n$.

Both steps cost $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ flops each, which is far cheaper than the $\frac{2}{3}n^3$ flops needed for the factorization itself.

### Why it helps

Solving $\mathbf{A}x = b$ directly requires $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ operations every time. With LU, the factorization costs $\frac{2}{3}n^3$ flops once, and then each new right-hand side $b$ requires only the two triangular substitutions described above at $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ flops each. This is directly relevant to control: the Newton systems that arise in MPC and trajectory optimization require solving the same coefficient matrix against multiple right-hand sides at each iteration. Factoring once and solving repeatedly is what makes that affordable.

### Banded matrices

A matrix $\mathbf{A}$ is called **banded** with **bandwidth** $k$ if all nonzero entries lie within $k$ diagonals of the main diagonal, that is $A_{ij} = 0$ whenever $|i - j| > k$. The bandwidth $k$ measures how far the nonzero entries spread away from the diagonal. When $k \ll n$, the matrix is **sparse** in a structured way, and the LU factors preserve this band structure. The factorization then costs $\mathcal{O}(nk^2)$ instead of $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$, a dramatic speedup. State-space matrices and finite-difference discretizations often have this structure.

## Cholesky Factorization

### What it is

When $\mathbf{A}$ is **symmetric positive definite** (SPD), Gaussian elimination can be made exactly twice as efficient by exploiting the symmetry. The result is the **Cholesky factorization**:

$$
\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^T,
$$

where $\mathbf{L}$ is lower triangular with positive diagonal entries. No permutation is needed for numerical stability because the SPD property guarantees it.

The cost is $\frac{1}{3}n^3$ flops, exactly **half** that of a general LU factorization (which costs $\frac{2}{3}n^3$ flops). This factor-of-two saving comes from processing only the lower triangle of $\mathbf{A}$, since the upper triangle is determined by symmetry.

### Solving linear systems with Cholesky

Once $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^T$, solving $\mathbf{A}x = b$ splits into two triangular solves. Substituting the factorization gives $\mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^T x = b$. Introducing $z = \mathbf{L}^T x$ yields the forward solve $\mathbf{L}z = b$ followed by the back solve $\mathbf{L}^T x = z$. Since $\mathbf{L}$ and $\mathbf{L}^T$ are triangular, both steps cost $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ flops. The benefit over LU is that only one triangular factor needs to be stored and applied (since $\mathbf{L}^T$ is just the transpose of $\mathbf{L}$), and the algorithm is numerically stable without pivoting.

### Why it comes up everywhere

SPD matrices appear naturally whenever you form $\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A}$ (least-squares normal equations), whenever you have a covariance matrix, or whenever a Lyapunov equation has a positive definite solution. In all these cases, Cholesky is the go-to solver.

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Cholesky Existence"}
A symmetric matrix $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{S}^n$ is positive definite if and only if its Cholesky factorization $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^T$ exists with all diagonal elements $L_{ii} > 0$. This provides an efficient positive-definiteness test: run the algorithm and check for breakdown.
:::

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Least-Squares via Cholesky and the Condition Number Penalty"}
Given $\mathbf{J} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ with $m \geq n$ and $\operatorname{rank}(\mathbf{J}) = n$, the normal equations
$$
\mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{J}\, x = \mathbf{J}^T y
$$
have a symmetric positive definite coefficient matrix $\mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{J}$. A Cholesky factorization $\mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{J} = \mathbf{R}^T \mathbf{R}$ turns this into two triangular solves: first $\mathbf{R}^T z = \mathbf{J}^T y$ (forward substitution), then $\mathbf{R} x = z$ (back substitution).

However, forming $\mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{J}$ explicitly has a significant drawback: the condition number satisfies $\kappa(\mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{J}) = \kappa(\mathbf{J})^2$. This means that if $\mathbf{J}$ has condition number $10^4$, the normal equations have condition number $10^8$, squaring the numerical sensitivity and potentially causing large errors in the computed solution. A relative perturbation of $\epsilon$ in $y$ can then cause a relative change of up to $\kappa(\mathbf{J})^2 \cdot \epsilon$ in $x$. For ill-conditioned $\mathbf{J}$, QR or SVD are preferable because they avoid this squaring effect.
:::

## QR Factorization

### What it is

Any matrix $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ (with $m \geq n$) can be decomposed as

$$
\mathbf{A}\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{R} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{Q}_1 & \mathbf{Q}_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{R} \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},
$$

where

- $\mathbf{P}$ is a column-permutation matrix (used for numerical stability; it reorders the columns of $\mathbf{A}$ before factoring),
- $\mathbf{Q} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ is **orthogonal** ($\mathbf{Q}^T \mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{I}$),
- $\mathbf{R} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is **upper triangular** with positive diagonal.

$\mathbf{Q}_1$ contains the first $n$ columns of $\mathbf{Q}$, and together they span the column space of $\mathbf{A}$. The QR factorization is typically computed via **Householder transformations** or **Givens rotations**.

When the full factorization $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{R}$ is written without the permutation (for example in theoretical statements or when no pivoting is used), it is written as $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{R}$ or in thin form as $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{Q}_1 \mathbf{R}$. In practice, the permutation is often included for numerical stability but absorbed into the notation or omitted when discussing properties of the factorization itself.

### The key geometric insight

Orthogonal matrices preserve Euclidean norms: $\|\mathbf{Q}v\|_2 = \|v\|_2$. This means multiplying by $\mathbf{Q}$ does not amplify errors. It is this property that makes QR numerically superior to the normal equations:

$$
\|\mathbf{A}x - b\|_2 = \|\mathbf{Q}^T(\mathbf{A}x - b)\|_2 = \left\| \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{R} \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} x - \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{Q}_1^T b \\ \mathbf{Q}_2^T b \end{bmatrix} \right\|_2.
$$

The minimum is achieved by solving the triangular system $\mathbf{R}x = \mathbf{Q}_1^T b$. The condition number of $\mathbf{R}$ equals that of $\mathbf{A}$ (not $\kappa(\mathbf{A})^2$ as with the normal equations). In practice, QR is the standard method for least-squares problems of moderate size.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — QR Factorization"}
For $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ with $m \geq n$ and $\operatorname{rank}(\mathbf{A}) = n$, the (thin) QR factorization without permutation is
$$
\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{Q}_1 \mathbf{R},
$$
where $\mathbf{Q}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ has orthonormal columns ($\mathbf{Q}_1^T \mathbf{Q}_1 = \mathbf{I}_n$) and $\mathbf{R} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is upper triangular with positive diagonal. Cost: $\approx 2mn^2 - \frac{2}{3}n^3$ flops.
:::

### Connection to Cholesky

The $\mathbf{R}$ factor in the QR factorization is exactly the Cholesky factor of $\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A}$. To see why, substitute the (unpivoted) QR factorization $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{Q}_1 \mathbf{R}$ directly:
$$
\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A} = (\mathbf{Q}_1\mathbf{R})^T(\mathbf{Q}_1\mathbf{R}) = \mathbf{R}^T \mathbf{Q}_1^T \mathbf{Q}_1 \mathbf{R} = \mathbf{R}^T \mathbf{R}.
$$
The key step is that $\mathbf{Q}_1^T \mathbf{Q}_1 = \mathbf{I}$, which holds because $\mathbf{Q}_1$ has orthonormal columns by construction. The result shows that QR is a numerically stable way to obtain the Cholesky factor of $\mathbf{A}^T\mathbf{A}$ without ever forming $\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A}$ explicitly. This is precisely the numerical advantage over the normal-equations approach: computing $\mathbf{A}^T\mathbf{A}$ first and then applying Cholesky squares the condition number, whereas QR avoids this by working directly with $\mathbf{A}$.

## Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

### What it is

The SVD is the most informative (and most expensive) of the standard factorizations. Every matrix $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ can be written as

$$
\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{U} \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{S} \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{V}^T = \mathbf{U}_1 \mathbf{S} \mathbf{V}^T,
$$

where

- $\mathbf{U} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ and $\mathbf{V} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ are **orthogonal**,
- $\mathbf{S} = \operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \ldots, \sigma_n)$ with $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_n \geq 0$.

The $\sigma_i$ are called the **singular values** of $\mathbf{A}$. The columns of $\mathbf{U}$ and $\mathbf{V}$ are the **left** and **right singular vectors**, respectively.

### Geometric interpretation

Every linear map $x \mapsto \mathbf{A}x$ can be decomposed into three operations:

1. **Rotate** (or reflect) the input via $\mathbf{V}^T$,
2. **Scale** along the principal axes by $\sigma_i$,
3. **Rotate** (or reflect) the output via $\mathbf{U}$.

This is the fundamental geometric picture. The singular values measure how much $\mathbf{A}$ stretches or compresses space in each principal direction. A large ratio $\sigma_1 / \sigma_n$ indicates the matrix is nearly rank-deficient in some directions and this ratio is precisely the condition number introduced at the beginning of this chapter:
$$
\kappa(\mathbf{A}) = \frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_n}.
$$

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — SVD and Rank"}
The rank of $\mathbf{A}$ equals the number of nonzero singular values. Intuitively, each nonzero singular value $\sigma_i$ corresponds to an independent direction in which $\mathbf{A}$ acts nontrivially. Directions with $\sigma_i = 0$ are in the null space of $\mathbf{A}$ and contribute nothing to the image.

The best rank-$k$ approximation to $\mathbf{A}$ in the Euclidean norm is
$$
\mathbf{A}_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i u_i v_i^T,
$$
and the approximation error is $\|\mathbf{A} - \mathbf{A}_k\|_2 = \sigma_{k+1}$. This result (the Eckart–Young theorem) is the foundation of model reduction in control.
:::

SVD is the only factorization that reliably identifies rank and low-rank structure. In contrast, LU can break down or give misleading results for nearly rank-deficient matrices. QR with column pivoting only partially reveals rank through the diagonal entries of $\mathbf{R}$ (which decay but may not be exactly zero). SVD provides all singular values directly, from which rank and the structure of the null space are read off unambiguously.

### SVD for least-squares

Substituting the SVD into $\min_x \|\mathbf{A}x - b\|_2^2$ gives
$$
x^* = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{u_i^T b}{\sigma_i} v_i.
$$

This formula is illuminating: if $\sigma_i$ is small, the coefficient $(u_i^T b) / \sigma_i$ can be very large, making $x^*$ sensitive to noise in $b$ in the $v_i$ direction (exactly what the condition number measures globally). When $\mathbf{A}$ is rank-deficient ($\sigma_n = 0$), the minimum-norm solution is obtained by simply omitting the terms with $\sigma_i = 0$. This motivates **truncated SVD** regularization in system identification.

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Condition Number and Sensitivity"}
Suppose $\mathbf{A}$ has singular values $\sigma_1 = 100$ and $\sigma_2 = 0.01$. The condition number is $\kappa = \sigma_1 / \sigma_2 = 10^4$. This means a relative perturbation of $10^{-4}$ in $b$ can cause a relative change of order $1$ in $x^*$, so the solution is essentially unreliable with standard double-precision arithmetic (which has round-off around $10^{-16}$).

The SVD identifies exactly which directions are problematic: those aligned with $v_2$, the right singular vector corresponding to $\sigma_2 = 0.01$ (the direction of small gain). Any component of $b$ projected onto $u_2$ is amplified by a factor of $1 / \sigma_2 = 100$ in the solution.
:::

## Comparing the Four Factorizations

| | LU | Cholesky | QR | SVD |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Requirement on $\mathbf{A}$** | Nonsingular | SPD | Any $m \geq n$ | Any $m,n$ |
| **Flop count** | $\tfrac{2}{3}n^3$ | $\tfrac{1}{3}n^3$ | $\approx 2mn^2$ | $\approx 2mn^2 + 11n^3$ |
| **Condition number preserved?** | Yes | No (squaring via $\mathbf{A}^T\mathbf{A}$) | Yes | Yes |
| **Reveals rank?** | No | No (via breakdown) | Partially | Yes |
| **Primary use** | General linear systems | SPD systems, covariance | Least-squares, orthogonalization | Sensitivity, rank, model reduction |

The **Reveals rank** row deserves a note: knowing whether a matrix has full rank is important to determine whether a least-squares problem has a unique solution or whether a system is identifiable. LU cannot detect rank deficiency reliably. QR with column pivoting produces diagonal entries $R_{11} \geq R_{22} \geq \cdots$ that tend to zero for rank-deficient matrices, giving a partial indicator. SVD directly provides all singular values, from which rank and the structure of the null space are read off unambiguously.

## Block Elimination and the Schur Complement

The factorizations above treat $\mathbf{A}$ as a single dense object. Many matrices in control and optimization have **block structure**, meaning that $\mathbf{A}$ is naturally partitioned into submatrices (blocks) that correspond to different subsystems, time steps, or variable groups. Exploiting this structure avoids treating every entry uniformly and leads to much more efficient algorithms. Block elimination is the extension of Gaussian elimination to the block level: instead of eliminating scalar entries, one eliminates entire block rows at once.

For a partitioned system with unknowns split as $x = (x_1, x_2)$, the coefficient matrix takes the form
$$
\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{A}_{11} & \mathbf{A}_{12} \\ \mathbf{A}_{21} & \mathbf{A}_{22} \end{bmatrix},
$$
where $x_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{n_1}$ collects the first group of unknowns and $x_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{n_2}$ the second. Eliminating the block $\mathbf{A}_{11}$ from the second block row (by subtracting $\mathbf{A}_{21}\mathbf{A}_{11}^{-1}$ times the first block row) yields a smaller system in the **Schur complement**
$$
\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{A}_{22} - \mathbf{A}_{21} \mathbf{A}_{11}^{-1} \mathbf{A}_{12}.
$$

The full system $\mathbf{A}x = b$ (with $b = (b_1, b_2)$) reduces to $\mathbf{S}x_2 = b_2 - \mathbf{A}_{21} \mathbf{A}_{11}^{-1} b_1$, followed by back-substitution $\mathbf{A}_{11} x_1 = b_1 - \mathbf{A}_{12} x_2$ for the first group of unknowns $x_1$. This is the computational backbone of many control algorithms:

- **KKT systems** in constrained optimization and MPC have exactly this block structure, with $\mathbf{A}_{11}$ being the Hessian and $\mathbf{A}_{12}$ holding the constraint gradients.
- **Riccati recursion** in LQR can be seen as successive Schur complement eliminations moving backwards through the time horizon.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Schur Complement"}
For a block matrix with $\mathbf{A}_{11}$ invertible, the Schur complement of $\mathbf{A}_{11}$ in $\mathbf{A}$ is
$$
\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{A}_{11}) = \mathbf{A}_{22} - \mathbf{A}_{21} \mathbf{A}_{11}^{-1} \mathbf{A}_{12}.
$$
$\mathbf{A}$ is positive definite if and only if $\mathbf{A}_{11} \succ 0$ and $\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{A}_{11}) \succ 0$.
:::

## Why Factorizations Matter for Control

Matrix factorizations are not abstract — they are the computational substrate of nearly every algorithm covered later in this course:

- **Kalman filter**: The covariance update $\mathbf{P}_{k|k} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K}_k \mathbf{C}) \mathbf{P}_{k|k-1}$ involves SPD matrices. In practice the **square-root Kalman filter** propagates the Cholesky factor of $\mathbf{P}$ directly, preventing numerical loss of positive definiteness.
- **System identification** (Ch. 9): Fitting model parameters to data is a least-squares problem. QR and SVD determine which model directions are well-identified from the data and which are not.
- **Linear MPC** (Ch. 14): Each iteration solves a structured QP whose KKT system has block structure. Cholesky and block elimination give the core computational speedup.
- **Model reduction** (Ch. 11): Balanced truncation computes the Gramians and their Cholesky factors. The Hankel singular values are obtained from the SVD of the product $\mathbf{L}_c \mathbf{L}_o^T$.

::: {.callout-warning title="Exercise — Choose the Right Factorization"}
For each scenario below, state which factorization is most appropriate and why:

1. Solving $\mathbf{A}x = b$ where $\mathbf{A}$ is a dense, general $200 \times 200$ matrix that will be reused with 50 different right-hand sides.
2. Solving the normal equations $\mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{J}\, x = \mathbf{J}^T y$ where $\mathbf{J}$ is a well-conditioned $500 \times 10$ matrix.
3. Finding the minimum-norm solution to $\mathbf{A}x = b$ where $\mathbf{A}$ is $3 \times 10$ and potentially rank-deficient.
4. Checking whether a $50 \times 50$ covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ estimated from data is numerically positive definite.
:::

::: {.callout-caution collapse="true" title="Solution — Show solution"}
1. **LU factorization**: Factor once ($\tfrac{2}{3}n^3$ flops), then each of the 50 solves costs only $2n^2$ flops.
2. **QR factorization of $\mathbf{J}$**: Avoids forming $\mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{J}$ and squaring the condition number. $\mathbf{R} x = \mathbf{Q}_1^T y$ gives the solution with condition number $\kappa(\mathbf{J})$ rather than $\kappa(\mathbf{J})^2$.
3. **SVD**: Identifies the null space directions (zero or tiny singular values) and returns the minimum-norm solution via $x^* = \sum_{\sigma_i > \epsilon} (u_i^T b / \sigma_i)\, v_i$.
4. **Cholesky**: Attempt the factorization. Successful completion with all $L_{ii} > 0$ confirms positive definiteness. A breakdown signals numerical indefiniteness.
:::
