# Matrix Factorizations

A matrix factorization rewrites a matrix \(A\) as a product of simpler, structured matrices — triangular, orthogonal, or diagonal — so that subsequent computations like solving linear systems, inverting matrices, or finding least-squares solutions become cheap and numerically well-behaved.

Rather than working with \(A\) directly, the pattern is always the same:

1. **Factor** \(A\) into structured pieces (done once, costs \(\mathcal{O}(n^3)\)).
2. **Solve or apply** the pieces to any right-hand side (repeated, costs \(\mathcal{O}(n^2)\)).

This *factor–solve* strategy is how almost every numerical algorithm in this course — from state estimation (Kalman filter) to model predictive control — solves linear systems under the hood.

---

## LU Factorization

### What it is

Every nonsingular matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) can be decomposed as

$$
PA = LU,
$$

where

- \(P\) is a **permutation matrix** (reorders rows to improve numerical stability),
- \(L\) is **unit lower triangular** (ones on diagonal, zeros above),
- \(U\) is **upper triangular** (zeros below diagonal).

This factorization is computed by **Gaussian elimination with partial pivoting**.

### Why it helps

Solving \(Ax = b\) directly requires \(\mathcal{O}(n^3)\) operations every time. With LU, the factorization costs \(\frac{2}{3}n^3\) flops once, and then each new right-hand side \(b\) requires only two triangular substitutions at \(n^2\) flops:

$$
PA = LU \quad \Rightarrow \quad
\underbrace{Lz = Pb}_{\text{forward substitution}} \quad \text{then} \quad
\underbrace{Ux = z}_{\text{back substitution}}
$$

This is directly relevant to control: the Newton systems that arise in MPC and trajectory optimization require solving the same coefficient matrix against multiple right-hand sides at each iteration. Factoring once and solving repeatedly is what makes that affordable.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — LU Factorization"}
For \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) nonsingular, the LU factorization is
$$
PA = LU,
$$
where \(P\) is a permutation matrix, \(L\) is unit lower triangular, and \(U\) is upper triangular. It always exists and can be computed in \(\frac{2}{3}n^3\) flops via Gaussian elimination with partial pivoting.
:::

### Banded and sparse cases

If \(A\) is **banded** with bandwidth \(k \ll n\), the LU factors preserve the band structure and the factorization costs \(\mathcal{O}(nk^2)\) instead of \(\mathcal{O}(n^3)\). State-space matrices and finite-difference discretizations often have this structure, making LU dramatically faster.

## Cholesky Factorization

### What it is

When \(A\) is **symmetric positive definite** (SPD), Gaussian elimination can be made exactly twice as efficient by exploiting the symmetry. The result is the **Cholesky factorization**:

$$
A = LL^T,
$$

where \(L\) is lower triangular with positive diagonal entries. No permutation is needed for numerical stability — the SPD property guarantees it.

The cost is \(\frac{1}{3}n^3\) flops, exactly half that of a general LU.

### Why it comes up everywhere

SPD matrices appear naturally whenever you form \(A^T A\) (least-squares normal equations), whenever you have a covariance matrix, or whenever a Lyapunov equation has a positive definite solution. In all these cases, Cholesky is the go-to solver.

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Cholesky Existence"}
A symmetric matrix \(A \in \mathbb{S}^n\) is positive definite if and only if its Cholesky factorization \(A = LL^T\) exists with all diagonal elements \(L_{ii} > 0\). This provides an efficient positive-definiteness test: run the algorithm, check for breakdown.
:::

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Least-Squares via Cholesky"}
Given \(J \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) with \(m \geq n\) and \(\operatorname{rank}(J) = n\), the normal equations
$$
J^T J\, x = J^T y
$$
have a symmetric positive definite coefficient matrix \(J^T J\). A Cholesky factorization \(J^T J = R^T R\) turns this into two triangular solves. One drawback: the condition number of \(J^T J\) is \(\kappa(J)^2\), which squares the numerical sensitivity of \(J\). For ill-conditioned \(J\), QR or SVD are preferable.
:::

## QR Factorization

### What it is

Any matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) (with \(m \geq n\)) can be decomposed as

$$
AP = QR = \begin{bmatrix} Q_1 & Q_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} R \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},
$$

where

- \(P\) is a column-permutation matrix,
- \(Q \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}\) is **orthogonal** (\(Q^T Q = I\)),
- \(R \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is **upper triangular** with positive diagonal.

\(Q_1\) contains the first \(n\) columns of \(Q\), and together they span the column space of \(A\). The QR factorization is typically computed via **Householder transformations** or **Givens rotations**.

### The key geometric insight

Orthogonal matrices preserve Euclidean norms: \(\|Qv\|_2 = \|v\|_2\). This means multiplying by \(Q\) does not amplify errors. It is this property that makes QR numerically superior to the normal equations:

$$
\|Ax - b\|_2 = \|Q^T(Ax - b)\|_2 = \left\| \begin{bmatrix} R \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} x - \begin{bmatrix} Q_1^T b \\ Q_2^T b \end{bmatrix} \right\|_2.
$$

The minimum is achieved by solving the triangular system \(Rx = Q_1^T b\). The condition number of \(R\) equals that of \(A\) — not \(\kappa(A)^2\) as with the normal equations. In practice, QR is the standard method for least-squares problems of moderate size.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — QR Factorization"}
For \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) with \(m \geq n\) and \(\operatorname{rank}(A) = n\), the (thin) QR factorization is
$$
A = Q_1 R,
$$
where \(Q_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) has orthonormal columns (\(Q_1^T Q_1 = I_n\)) and \(R \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is upper triangular with positive diagonal. Cost: \(\approx 2mn^2 - \frac{2}{3}n^3\) flops.
:::

### Connection to Cholesky

The \(R\) factor in the QR factorization is exactly the Cholesky factor of \(A^T A\):
$$
A^T A = (QR)^T(QR) = R^T Q^T Q R = R^T R.
$$

This means QR is a numerically stable way to obtain the Cholesky factor without forming \(A^T A\) explicitly.

## Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

### What it is

The SVD is the most informative — and most expensive — of the standard factorizations. Every matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) can be written as

$$
A = U \begin{bmatrix} S \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} V^T = U_1 S V^T,
$$

where

- \(U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}\) and \(V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) are **orthogonal**,
- \(S = \operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \ldots, \sigma_n)\) with \(\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_n \geq 0\).

The \(\sigma_i\) are called the **singular values** of \(A\). The columns of \(U\) and \(V\) are the **left** and **right singular vectors**, respectively.

### Geometric interpretation

Every linear map \(x \mapsto Ax\) can be decomposed into three operations:

1. **Rotate** (or reflect) the input via \(V^T\),
2. **Scale** along the principal axes by \(\sigma_i\),
3. **Rotate** (or reflect) the output via \(U\).

This is the fundamental geometric picture. The singular values measure how much \(A\) stretches or compresses space in each principal direction. A large ratio \(\sigma_1 / \sigma_n\) indicates the matrix is nearly rank-deficient in some directions — the condition number is exactly
$$
\kappa(A) = \frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_n}.
$$

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — SVD and Rank"}
The rank of \(A\) equals the number of nonzero singular values. The best rank-\(k\) approximation to \(A\) in the Euclidean norm is
$$
A_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i u_i v_i^T,
$$
and the approximation error is \(\|A - A_k\|_2 = \sigma_{k+1}\). This result (the Eckart–Young theorem) is the foundation of model reduction in control.
:::

### SVD for least-squares

Substituting the SVD into \(\min_x \|Ax - b\|_2^2\) gives
$$
x^* = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{u_i^T b}{\sigma_i} v_i.
$$

This formula is illuminating: if \(\sigma_i\) is small, the coefficient \((u_i^T b) / \sigma_i\) can be very large, making \(x^*\) sensitive to noise in \(b\) in the \(v_i\) direction. When \(A\) is rank-deficient (\(\sigma_n = 0\)), the minimum-norm solution is obtained by simply omitting the terms with \(\sigma_i = 0\). This motivates **truncated SVD** regularization in system identification.

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Condition Number and Sensitivity"}
Suppose \(A\) has singular values \(\sigma_1 = 100\) and \(\sigma_2 = 0.01\). The condition number is \(\kappa = 10^4\). This means a relative perturbation of \(10^{-4}\) in \(b\) can cause a relative change of order \(1\) in \(x^*\). The SVD identifies exactly which directions are problematic: those aligned with \(v_2\) (the direction of small gain).
:::

## Comparing the Four Factorizations

| | LU | Cholesky | QR | SVD |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Requirement on \(A\)** | Nonsingular | SPD | Any \(m \geq n\) | Any \(m,n\) |
| **Flop count** | \(\tfrac{2}{3}n^3\) | \(\tfrac{1}{3}n^3\) | \(\approx 2mn^2\) | \(\approx 2mn^2 + 11n^3\) |
| **Condition number preserved?** | Yes | No (squaring via \(A^TA\)) | Yes | Yes |
| **Reveals rank?** | No | No (via breakdown) | Partially | Yes |
| **Primary use** | General linear systems | SPD systems, covariance | Least-squares, orthogonalization | Sensitivity, rank, model reduction |

## Block Elimination and the Schur Complement

When the matrix \(A\) has block structure
$$
A = \begin{bmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} \\ A_{21} & A_{22} \end{bmatrix},
$$
it is often more efficient to first eliminate the block \(A_{11}\) and then solve a smaller system in the **Schur complement**
$$
S = A_{22} - A_{21} A_{11}^{-1} A_{12}.
$$

The system \(Ax = b\) reduces to \(Sx_2 = b_2 - A_{21} A_{11}^{-1} b_1\), followed by back-substitution for \(x_1\). This is the computational backbone of many control algorithms:

- **KKT systems** in constrained optimization and MPC have exactly this block structure, with \(A_{11}\) being the Hessian and \(A_{12}\) holding the constraint gradients.
- **Riccati recursion** in LQR can be seen as successive Schur complement eliminations moving backwards through the time horizon.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Schur Complement"}
For a block matrix with \(A_{11}\) invertible, the Schur complement of \(A_{11}\) in \(A\) is
$$
S(A_{11}) = A_{22} - A_{21} A_{11}^{-1} A_{12}.
$$
\(A\) is positive definite if and only if \(A_{11} \succ 0\) and \(S(A_{11}) \succ 0\).
:::

## Why Factorizations Matter for Control

Matrix factorizations are not abstract — they are the computational substrate of nearly every algorithm covered later in this course:

- **Kalman filter**: The covariance update \(P_{k|k} = (I - K_k C) P_{k|k-1}\) involves SPD matrices. In practice the **square-root Kalman filter** propagates the Cholesky factor of \(P\) directly, preventing numerical loss of positive definiteness.
- **System identification** (Ch. 9): Fitting model parameters to data is a least-squares problem; QR and SVD determine which model directions are well-identified from the data and which are not.
- **Linear MPC** (Ch. 14): Each iteration solves a structured QP whose KKT system has block structure; Cholesky and block elimination give the core computational speedup.
- **Model reduction** (Ch. 11): Balanced truncation computes the Gramians and their Cholesky factors; the Hankel singular values are obtained from the SVD of the product \(L_c L_o^T\).

::: {.callout-warning title="Exercise — Choose the Right Factorization"}
For each scenario below, state which factorization is most appropriate and why:

1. Solving \(Ax = b\) where \(A\) is a dense, general \(200 \times 200\) matrix that will be reused with 50 different right-hand sides.
2. Solving the normal equations \(J^T J\, x = J^T y\) where \(J\) is a well-conditioned \(500 \times 10\) matrix.
3. Finding the minimum-norm solution to \(Ax = b\) where \(A\) is \(3 \times 10\) and potentially rank-deficient.
4. Checking whether a \(50 \times 50\) covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) estimated from data is numerically positive definite.
:::

::: {.callout-caution collapse="true" title="Solution — Show solution"}
1. **LU factorization**: Factor once (\(\tfrac{2}{3}n^3\) flops), then each of the 50 solves costs only \(2n^2\) flops.
2. **QR factorization of \(J\)**: Avoids forming \(J^T J\) and squaring the condition number; \(R x = Q_1^T y\) gives the solution with condition number \(\kappa(J)\) rather than \(\kappa(J)^2\).
3. **SVD**: Identifies the null space directions (zero or tiny singular values) and returns the minimum-norm solution via \(x^* = \sum_{\sigma_i > \epsilon} (u_i^T b / \sigma_i)\, v_i\).
4. **Cholesky**: Attempt the factorization; successful completion with all \(L_{ii} > 0\) confirms positive definiteness. A breakdown signals numerical indefiniteness.
:::
