# 1.1 Linear Algebra {#sec-linear-algebra}

Linear algebra is the mathematical backbone of control systems theory.
Almost every concept — state-space models, stability analysis, controllability, observability — reduces to operations on vectors and matrices.
This chapter reviews the essential structures: vector spaces, linear maps, matrix properties, eigenvalues, and norms.

---

## Fields and Vector Spaces

A **field** is the algebraic structure that provides the scalars used to scale vectors. The two fields relevant for control are the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$ and the complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Field"}
A **field** $(\mathbb{F}, +, \cdot)$ is a commutative ring in which every non-zero element has a multiplicative inverse.
The most important examples for control are $\mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbb{C}$.
:::

A **vector space** packages the notion of adding elements and scaling them by field elements, subject to a minimal set of consistency axioms.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Vector Space"}
A **vector space** (or *linear space*) $(V, \mathbb{F})$ is a set $V$ of vectors together with a field $\mathbb{F}$ of scalars, equipped with two operations:

- **Vector addition** $+: V \times V \to V$
- **Scalar multiplication** $\cdot: \mathbb{F} \times V \to V$

such that the following **eight vector space axioms** hold for all $u, v, w \in V$ and $a, b \in \mathbb{F}$:

1. *Associativity of addition:* $(u + v) + w = u + (v + w)$
2. *Commutativity of addition:* $u + v = v + u$
3. *Additive identity:* $\exists\, \mathbf{0} \in V$ such that $v + \mathbf{0} = v$
4. *Additive inverse:* $\exists\, {-v} \in V$ such that $v + (-v) = \mathbf{0}$
5. *Associativity of scalar multiplication:* $a(bv) = (ab)v$
6. *Scalar identity:* $1 \cdot v = v$
7. *Distributivity over vector addition:* $a(u + v) = au + av$
8. *Distributivity over scalar addition:* $(a + b)v = av + bv$

A set with two operations is a vector space **if and only if** all eight axioms are satisfied.
:::

The most relevant instance for control is $\mathbb{R}^n$ over $\mathbb{R}$, which serves as the state, input, and output space of linear systems.

## Subspaces, Span, and Basis

Not every subset of a vector space is itself a vector space. A subset that inherits the vector space structure from its parent is called a subspace.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Subspace"}
A non-empty subset $W \subseteq V$ is a **subspace** of $(V, \mathbb{F})$ if it is **closed under vector addition and scalar multiplication**, i.e.:
$$
w_1, w_2 \in W,\; a_1, a_2 \in \mathbb{F} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad a_1 w_1 + a_2 w_2 \in W.
$$
Equivalently, $W$ must itself satisfy all vector space axioms under the operations inherited from $V$.
In particular, $\mathbf{0} \in W$ is a necessary condition (set $a_1 = a_2 = 0$).
:::

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^2$"}
In $\mathbb{R}^2$ the subspaces are exactly: $\{\mathbf{0}\}$, all lines through the origin, and $\mathbb{R}^2$ itself.
The set $\{(x_1, x_2) \mid x_2 = x_1 + 1\}$ is *not* a subspace — it does not contain $\mathbf{0}$ and is not closed under addition.
:::

The span and the concept of linear independence allow us to describe which vectors are reachable by linear combinations, and whether a description is minimal.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Span and Linear Independence"}
The **span** of a set $S \subseteq V$ is
$$
\operatorname{Span}(S) = \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i v_i \;\ \Bigg|\;\ n \in \mathbb{N},\; a_i \in \mathbb{F},\; v_i \in S \right\}.
$$
A set $S$ is **linearly independent** if the only solution to
$$
\sum_{i=1}^n a_i v_i = \mathbf{0}
$$
is $a_i = 0$ for all $i$.
:::

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Basis and Dimension"}
A set $\{b_1, \ldots, b_n\}$ is a **basis** of $V$ if it is linearly independent and $\operatorname{Span}(\{b_1,\ldots,b_n\}) = V$.
If such a finite basis exists, $n$ is the **dimension** of $V$, written $\dim V = n$.
Every basis of a finite-dimensional space has the same number of elements.
:::

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Canonical Basis of $\mathbb{R}^3$"}
The standard basis $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\} = \{(1,0,0),\,(0,1,0),\,(0,0,1)\}$ is a basis for $\mathbb{R}^3$ with $\dim \mathbb{R}^3 = 3$.
Any three linearly independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$ also form a valid basis.
:::

## Linear Maps and Matrix Representation

Linear maps are the structure-preserving functions between vector spaces. Once bases are fixed, they are concretely represented as matrices.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Linear Map"}
A function $A: U \to V$ between two vector spaces over the same field $\mathbb{F}$ is **linear** if
$$
A(a_1 u_1 + a_2 u_2) = a_1 A(u_1) + a_2 A(u_2) \quad \forall\, u_1, u_2 \in U,\; a_1, a_2 \in \mathbb{F}.
$$
:::

Once bases are fixed for $U$ (dimension $n$) and $V$ (dimension $m$), every linear map $A: U \to V$ has a unique **matrix representation** $A \in \mathbb{F}^{m \times n}$ such that the action of $A$ corresponds to matrix-vector multiplication.

Two important associated subspaces capture the input-output structure of a linear map:

- **Null space**: $\operatorname{Null}(A) = \{u \in U \mid Au = \mathbf{0}\}$, a subspace of $U$.
- **Range space**: $\operatorname{Range}(A) = \{Au \mid u \in U\}$, a subspace of $V$.

### Computing Rank, Null Space, and Range

In practice, all three quantities are computed via **row reduction** (Gaussian elimination) of $A$:

1. Bring $A$ to **row echelon form** (or reduced row echelon form, RREF).
2. The number of **pivot columns** equals $\operatorname{rank}(A)$.
3. The **range** of $A$ is spanned by the pivot columns of the *original* matrix $A$ (not the RREF).
4. The **null space** is found by solving $Ax = 0$ using back-substitution; the free variables parameterise the solution. $\operatorname{nullity}(A) = n - \operatorname{rank}(A)$.

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Rank and Null Space"}
Let
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 & 3\\ 2 & 4 & 6\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Row reduction gives one pivot (column 1), so $\operatorname{rank}(A) = 1$ and $\operatorname{nullity}(A) = 3 - 1 = 2$.
The null space is spanned by $\{(-2,1,0)^\top,\, (-3,0,1)^\top\}$, and the range is $\operatorname{Span}\{(1,2)^\top\}$.
:::

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Rank-Nullity"}
For any matrix $A \in \mathbb{F}^{m \times n}$:
$$
\operatorname{rank}(A) + \operatorname{nullity}(A) = n,
$$
where $\operatorname{rank}(A) = \dim\,\operatorname{Range}(A)$ and $\operatorname{nullity}(A) = \dim\,\operatorname{Null}(A)$.

Additionally, $0 \leq \operatorname{rank}(A) \leq \min(m, n)$.
:::

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Invertibility of Square Matrices"}
For $A \in \mathbb{F}^{n \times n}$, the following are equivalent:

1. $A$ is invertible ($\det A \neq 0$).
2. $A$ is bijective as a linear map.
3. $\operatorname{rank}(A) = n$.
4. $\operatorname{nullity}(A) = 0$.
5. The columns (and rows) of $A$ are linearly independent.
6. None of the eigenvalues of $A$ equals zero.
:::

## Change of Basis

When the same linear map is expressed in a different coordinate system (basis), its matrix representation changes according to a similarity transformation. The eigenvalues, however, are a property of the map itself and remain unchanged.

If $A \in \mathbb{F}^{n \times n}$ is the matrix representation in one basis and $Q \in \mathbb{F}^{n \times n}$ is the invertible change-of-basis matrix, then the representation in the new basis is:
$$
\tilde{A} = Q^{-1} A Q.
$$
Matrices related this way are called **similar** and share the same eigenvalues (spectrum).

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Change of Basis in $\mathbb{R}^2$"}
Let $A$ be represented in the canonical basis $\{e_1, e_2\}$ and let
$$
Q = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}
$$
be the change-of-basis matrix to the new basis $\{e_1,\, e_1+e_2\}$.
The new representation is $\tilde{A} = Q^{-1}AQ$.
:::

## Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors identify the directions along which a linear map acts as a simple scaling. They are central to stability analysis and the computation of the matrix exponential.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Eigenvalue and Eigenvector"}
A scalar $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ is an **eigenvalue** of $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ if there exists a non-zero vector $v \in \mathbb{C}^n$ (the **eigenvector**) such that
$$
Av = \lambda v \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (A - \lambda I)v = 0.
$$
:::

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Characteristic Polynomial"}
The eigenvalues of $A$ are the roots of the **characteristic polynomial**:
$$
p_A(\lambda) = \det(\lambda I - A) = \lambda^n + c_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1} + \cdots + c_0.
$$
Equivalently, $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue if and only if $\det(\lambda I - A) = 0$.
:::

Two multiplicities matter:

- **Algebraic multiplicity**: number of times $\lambda$ appears as a root of $p_A(\lambda)$.
- **Geometric multiplicity**: $\dim\,\operatorname{Null}(A - \lambda I)$, i.e., the number of linearly independent eigenvectors for $\lambda$.

The geometric multiplicity is always $\leq$ the algebraic multiplicity. When they are equal for every eigenvalue, $A$ is called **diagonalisable**.

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Diagonalisation"}
$A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is **diagonalisable** if and only if its eigenvectors $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ form a basis of $\mathbb{C}^n$. Letting $T = [v_1 \mid \cdots \mid v_n]$:
$$
A = T \Lambda T^{-1}, \quad \Lambda = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n).
$$
All matrices with **distinct eigenvalues** are diagonalisable.
:::

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Eigenvalues of a 2x2 Matrix"}
Consider
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\-1 & 0\end{pmatrix}.
$$
The characteristic polynomial is $\det(\lambda I - A) = \lambda^2 + 1$, giving eigenvalues $\lambda_{1,2} = \pm j$.

The corresponding eigenvectors are
$$
v_1 = c\begin{pmatrix}1\\j\end{pmatrix}, \quad v_2 = c\begin{pmatrix}1\\-j\end{pmatrix}, \quad c \neq 0.
$$
Since the eigenvalues are distinct, $A$ is diagonalisable.
:::

## Jordan Normal Form

When $A$ is **not** diagonalisable (geometric multiplicity $<$ algebraic multiplicity for some eigenvalue), $A$ cannot be brought to a diagonal form. The most structured form still achievable via a similarity transformation is the **Jordan normal form**.

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Jordan Decomposition"}
For any $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ there exists an invertible $V \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ such that
$$
A = V J V^{-1}, \qquad
J = \operatorname{blockdiag}(J_1, J_2, \ldots, J_k),
$$
where each **Jordan block** is
$$
J_i = \begin{pmatrix}\lambda_i & 1 & & \\ & \lambda_i & \ddots & \\ & & \ddots & 1 \\ & & & \lambda_i\end{pmatrix} \in \mathbb{C}^{n_i \times n_i}.
$$
If all Jordan blocks are $1 \times 1$, $J = \Lambda$ and $A$ is diagonalisable.
:::

### Computing the Matrix Exponential via Jordan Form

The Jordan form gives a systematic way to compute $e^{At}$, the state-transition matrix of $\dot{x} = Ax$.

Starting from $A = VJV^{-1}$, we obtain:
$$
e^{At} = V e^{Jt} V^{-1}, \qquad e^{Jt} = \operatorname{blockdiag}(e^{J_1 t}, \ldots, e^{J_k t}).
$$
For a single Jordan block $J_i$ of size $n_i$ with eigenvalue $\lambda_i$, write $J_i = \lambda_i I + N$ where $N$ is the nilpotent shift matrix ($N^{n_i} = 0$). Since $\lambda_i I$ and $N$ commute:
$$
e^{J_i t} = e^{\lambda_i t} e^{Nt} = e^{\lambda_i t} \sum_{k=0}^{n_i - 1} \frac{t^k}{k!} N^k
= e^{\lambda_i t}
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & t & \tfrac{t^2}{2!} & \cdots & \tfrac{t^{n_i-1}}{(n_i-1)!} \\
0 & 1 & t & \cdots & \tfrac{t^{n_i-2}}{(n_i-2)!} \\
\vdots & & \ddots & & \vdots \\
0 & \cdots & 0 & 1 & t \\
0 & \cdots & & 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$
For a diagonalisable matrix (all blocks $1 \times 1$), this reduces to the familiar $e^{At} = T \operatorname{diag}(e^{\lambda_i t}) T^{-1}$.

### Stability Implication of Repeated Imaginary Eigenvalues

The polynomial factors $t^k$ in $e^{J_i t}$ have a direct stability consequence. Consider an eigenvalue $\lambda_i = j\omega$ (purely imaginary) with a Jordan block of size $n_i \geq 2$. The corresponding response contains terms like $t \cdot e^{j\omega t}$. Because $|e^{j\omega t}| = 1$ for all $t$ but $t \to \infty$, the overall term $t \cdot e^{j\omega t}$ is **unbounded**. Therefore, a repeated eigenvalue on the imaginary axis causes the state to grow without bound even though the eigenvalue itself has zero real part. A diagonalisable matrix with purely imaginary eigenvalues, by contrast, produces only bounded oscillations.

## Norms

Vector spaces carry purely algebraic structure. To speak about *size*, *distance*, and *convergence*, we equip them with a **norm**.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Norm"}
A **norm** on a vector space $(V, \mathbb{F})$ with $\mathbb{F} \in \{\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}\}$ is a function $\|\cdot\|: V \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying

1. *Triangle inequality:* $\|v_1 + v_2\| \leq \|v_1\| + \|v_2\|$
2. *Absolute homogeneity:* $\|av\| = |a|\,\|v\|$
3. *Positive definiteness:* $\|v\| = 0 \Leftrightarrow v = \mathbf{0}$

A vector space equipped with a norm is called a **normed space**.
:::

For $x \in \mathbb{F}^n$, the most common norms are:

| Name | Formula |
|------|-------------------|
| 1-norm | $\|x\|_1 = \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|$ |
| Euclidean (2-norm) | $\|x\|_2 = \left(\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|^2\right)^{1/2}$ |
| $\infty$-norm | $\|x\|_\infty = \max_{i} |x_i|$ |

For matrices $A \in \mathbb{F}^{m \times n}$, the **induced (operator) norm** measures how much $A$ can amplify a vector:
$$
\|A\| = \sup_{x \neq 0} \frac{\|Ax\|}{\|x\|} = \sup_{\|x\|=1} \|Ax\|.
$$

All norms on a finite-dimensional space are **equivalent**: for any two norms $\|\cdot\|_a$ and $\|\cdot\|_b$ there exist $m_l, m_u > 0$ such that
$$
m_l \|x\|_a \leq \|x\|_b \leq m_u \|x\|_a \quad \forall\, x \in V.
$$
This means convergence and stability conclusions do not depend on the specific choice of norm in finite dimensions.

---

## Why This Matters for Control

These linear algebra concepts directly enable the core tools of linear control:

- **Eigenvalues** of the system matrix $A$ determine stability (see [Chapter 4](#sec-stability))
- **Jordan form** and matrix exponential govern the time evolution $e^{At}$ of state-space systems (see [Chapter 3](#sec-lti-solutions))
- **Rank conditions** on matrices built from $A$ and $B$ determine controllability (see [Chapter 8](#sec-controllability))
- **Null and range spaces** characterise which states are reachable or observable
- **Norms** provide the metric foundation for convergence, stability margins, and $H_\infty$ design

## Exercises

::: {.callout-warning title="Exercise"}
**1.** Let
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1\\0 & 3\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Compute the eigenvalues and eigenvectors. Is $A$ diagonalisable?

**2a.** Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ and $B \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$. State the rank condition under which the system $\dot{x} = Ax + Bu$ is controllable.

**2b.** Consider the numerical example
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\0 & 0\end{pmatrix}, \quad
B = \begin{pmatrix}0 \\ 1\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Is this system controllable? Form the controllability matrix and verify your answer.

**3.** Show that similar matrices have the same characteristic polynomial, and hence the same eigenvalues.
:::

::: {.callout-caution collapse="true" title="Solution — Show solution"}
**1.** Characteristic polynomial:
$$
\det(\lambda I - A) = (\lambda-2)(\lambda-3) = 0 \implies \lambda_1 = 2,\; \lambda_2 = 3.
$$
Distinct eigenvalues $\Rightarrow$ $A$ is diagonalisable. Eigenvectors:
$$
\lambda_1 = 2:\; v_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad
\lambda_2 = 3:\; v_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}.
$$

**2a.** The system is controllable if and only if
$$
\operatorname{rank}\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}B & AB & \cdots & A^{n-1}B\end{pmatrix}}_{=:\mathcal{C}} = n.
$$

**2b.** Compute the controllability matrix $\mathcal{C} = \begin{pmatrix}B & AB\end{pmatrix}$:
$$
AB = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathcal{C} = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix}.
$$
$\det(\mathcal{C}) = -1 \neq 0$, so $\operatorname{rank}(\mathcal{C}) = 2 = n$. The system **is controllable**.

**3.** Let $\tilde{A} = Q^{-1}AQ$. Then:
$$
\det(\lambda I - \tilde{A})
= \det\!\left(Q^{-1}(\lambda I - A)Q\right)
= \underbrace{\det(Q^{-1})\det(Q)}_{=1} \cdot \det(\lambda I - A)
= \det(\lambda I - A).
$$
:::
