# 1.1 Linear Algebra {#sec-linear-algebra}

Linear algebra is the mathematical backbone of control systems theory.
Almost every concept, from state-space models to stability analysis, controllability, and observability, reduces to operations on vectors and matrices.
This chapter builds the essential structures in a deliberate order: we start with the abstract notion of a vector space, then introduce the maps between such spaces, study the spectral properties of square matrices, and finally equip spaces with geometry through inner products and norms.
Throughout, we connect each algebraic concept to its geometric or systems-theoretic meaning so that the theory does not remain purely formal.

---

## Fields and Vector Spaces

A **field** is the algebraic structure that provides the scalars used to scale vectors. The two fields relevant for control are the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$ and the complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Field"}
A **field** $(\mathbb{F}, +, \cdot)$ is a commutative ring in which every non-zero element has a multiplicative inverse.
The most important examples for control are $\mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbb{C}$.
:::

A **vector space** packages the notion of adding elements and scaling them by field elements, subject to a minimal set of consistency axioms.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Vector Space"}
A **vector space** (or *linear space*) $(V, \mathbb{F})$ is a set $V$ of vectors together with a field $\mathbb{F}$ of scalars, equipped with two operations:

- **Vector addition** $+: V \times V \to V$
- **Scalar multiplication** $\cdot: \mathbb{F} \times V \to V$

such that the following **eight vector space axioms** hold for all $u, v, w \in V$ and $a, b \in \mathbb{F}$:

1. *Associativity of addition:* $(u + v) + w = u + (v + w)$
2. *Commutativity of addition:* $u + v = v + u$
3. *Additive identity:* $\exists\, \mathbf{0} \in V$ such that $v + \mathbf{0} = v$
4. *Additive inverse:* $\exists\, {-v} \in V$ such that $v + (-v) = \mathbf{0}$
5. *Associativity of scalar multiplication:* $a(bv) = (ab)v$
6. *Scalar identity:* $1 \cdot v = v$
7. *Distributivity over vector addition:* $a(u + v) = au + av$
8. *Distributivity over scalar addition:* $(a + b)v = av + bv$

A set with two operations is a vector space **if and only if** all eight axioms are satisfied.
:::

The most relevant instance for control is $\mathbb{R}^n$ over $\mathbb{R}$, which serves as the state, input, and output space of linear systems.

## Subspaces, Span, and Basis

A generic subset of a vector space need not itself form a vector space: it may fail to contain the zero vector or may not be closed under addition and scaling. A subset that inherits the full vector space structure from its parent is called a subspace.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Subspace"}
A non-empty subset $W \subseteq V$ is a **subspace** of $(V, \mathbb{F})$ if it is **closed under vector addition and scalar multiplication**, i.e.:
$$
w_1, w_2 \in W,\; a_1, a_2 \in \mathbb{F} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad a_1 w_1 + a_2 w_2 \in W.
$$
Equivalently, $W$ must itself satisfy all vector space axioms under the operations inherited from $V$.
In particular, $\mathbf{0} \in W$ is a necessary condition (set $a_1 = a_2 = 0$).
:::

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^2$"}
In $\mathbb{R}^2$ the subspaces are exactly:

- $\{\mathbf{0}\}$ (the origin alone)
- Every line through the origin, e.g. $\{(x_1, x_2) \mid x_2 = c\,x_1\}$ for any $c \in \mathbb{R}$
- $\mathbb{R}^2$ itself

The set $\{(x_1, x_2) \mid x_2 = x_1 + 1\}$ is *not* a subspace: it does not contain $\mathbf{0}$ and is not closed under addition.
:::

The span and the concept of linear independence allow us to describe which vectors are reachable by linear combinations, and whether a description is minimal.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Span and Linear Independence"}
**Span (intuition):** The span of a set of vectors is the collection of all points you can reach by forming any linear combination of those vectors. It is the smallest subspace containing the set.

Formally, the **span** of a set $S \subseteq V$ is
$$
\operatorname{Span}(S) = \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i v_i \;\ \Bigg|\;\ n \in \mathbb{N},\; a_i \in \mathbb{F},\; v_i \in S \right\}.
$$

**Linear independence (intuition):** A set of vectors is linearly independent if none of the vectors can be written as a combination of the others. In other words, each vector adds a genuinely new direction.

Formally, a set $S$ is **linearly independent** if the only solution to
$$
\sum_{i=1}^n a_i v_i = \mathbf{0}
$$
is $a_i = 0$ for all $i$.
:::

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Basis and Dimension"}
A set $\{b_1, \ldots, b_n\}$ is a **basis** of $V$ if it is linearly independent and $\operatorname{Span}(\{b_1,\ldots,b_n\}) = V$.
If such a finite basis exists, $n$ is the **dimension** of $V$, written $\dim V = n$.
Every basis of a finite-dimensional space has the same number of elements.
:::

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Canonical Basis of $\mathbb{R}^3$"}
The standard basis $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\} = \{(1,0,0),\,(0,1,0),\,(0,0,1)\}$ is a basis for $\mathbb{R}^3$ with $\dim \mathbb{R}^3 = 3$.
Any three linearly independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$ also form a valid basis.
:::

## Linear Maps and Matrix Representation

With the building blocks of vector spaces in place, we now ask: which functions between vector spaces are the "right" ones, i.e. the ones that respect the linear structure? These are the linear maps. Once bases are chosen, every linear map is fully captured by a matrix.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Linear Map"}
A function $A: U \to V$ between two vector spaces over the same field $\mathbb{F}$ is **linear** if
$$
A(a_1 u_1 + a_2 u_2) = a_1 A(u_1) + a_2 A(u_2) \quad \forall\, u_1, u_2 \in U,\; a_1, a_2 \in \mathbb{F}.
$$
:::

Once bases are fixed for $U$ (dimension $n$) and $V$ (dimension $m$), every linear map $A: U \to V$ has a unique **matrix representation** $A \in \mathbb{F}^{m \times n}$ such that the action of $A$ corresponds to matrix-vector multiplication.

Two important associated subspaces capture the input-output structure of a linear map:

- **Null space (kernel):** $\operatorname{Null}(A) = \{u \in U \mid Au = \mathbf{0}\}$, a subspace of $U$. *Intuition:* the null space contains all inputs that $A$ "kills" or maps to zero. Its dimension tells you how many degrees of freedom are invisible to $A$.
- **Range space (image):** $\operatorname{Range}(A) = \{Au \mid u \in U\}$, a subspace of $V$. *Intuition:* the range is the set of all outputs that $A$ can actually produce. In control, it tells you which states or outputs are reachable.

### Computing Rank, Null Space, and Range

In practice, all three quantities are computed via **row reduction** (Gaussian elimination) of $A$:

1. Bring $A$ to **row echelon form** (or reduced row echelon form, RREF).
2. The number of **pivot columns** equals $\operatorname{rank}(A)$.
3. The **range** of $A$ is spanned by the pivot columns of the *original* matrix $A$ (not the RREF).
4. The **null space** is found by solving $Ax = 0$ using back-substitution. The free variables parameterise the solution. $\operatorname{nullity}(A) = n - \operatorname{rank}(A)$.

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Rank and Null Space"}
Let
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 & 3\\ 2 & 4 & 6\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Row reduction gives one pivot (column 1), so $\operatorname{rank}(A) = 1$ and $\operatorname{nullity}(A) = 3 - 1 = 2$.
The null space is spanned by $\{(-2,1,0)^\top,\, (-3,0,1)^\top\}$, and the range is $\operatorname{Span}\{(1,2)^\top\}$.
:::

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Rank-Nullity"}
For any matrix $A \in \mathbb{F}^{m \times n}$:
$$
\operatorname{rank}(A) + \operatorname{nullity}(A) = n,
$$
where $\operatorname{rank}(A) = \dim\,\operatorname{Range}(A)$ and $\operatorname{nullity}(A) = \dim\,\operatorname{Null}(A)$.

Additionally, $0 \leq \operatorname{rank}(A) \leq \min(m, n)$.
:::

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Invertibility of Square Matrices"}
For $A \in \mathbb{F}^{n \times n}$, the following are equivalent:

1. $A$ is invertible ($\det A \neq 0$).
2. $A$ is bijective as a linear map (bijective means both injective, i.e. one-to-one, and surjective, i.e. onto).
3. $\operatorname{rank}(A) = n$.
4. $\operatorname{nullity}(A) = 0$.
5. The columns (and rows) of $A$ are linearly independent.
6. None of the eigenvalues of $A$ equals zero.
:::

## Change of Basis

A linear map is an abstract object. Its matrix representation depends on the chosen coordinate system (basis). When we switch to a different basis, the matrix changes according to a **similarity transformation**, yet the underlying map and its intrinsic properties remain unchanged.

If $A \in \mathbb{F}^{n \times n}$ is the matrix representation in one basis and $Q \in \mathbb{F}^{n \times n}$ is the invertible change-of-basis matrix, then the representation in the new basis is:
$$
\tilde{A} = Q^{-1} A Q.
$$
Matrices related this way are called **similar** and share the same eigenvalues (spectrum).

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Change of Basis in $\mathbb{R}^2$"}
Let $A$ be represented in the canonical basis $\{e_1, e_2\}$ and let
$$
Q = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}
$$
be the change-of-basis matrix to the new basis $\{e_1,\, e_1+e_2\}$.
The new representation is $\tilde{A} = Q^{-1}AQ$.
While the entries of $\tilde{A}$ differ from those of $A$, both matrices describe the exact same linear transformation; they are merely viewed from different coordinate frames.
:::

## Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors reveal the "natural directions" of a linear map, i.e. the directions along which the map acts as a pure scaling. Understanding these directions is essential for stability analysis, for diagonalizing a system, and for computing the matrix exponential that drives the time evolution of state-space systems.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Eigenvalue and Eigenvector"}
A scalar $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ is an **eigenvalue** of $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ if there exists a non-zero vector $v \in \mathbb{C}^n$ (the **eigenvector**) such that
$$
Av = \lambda v \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (A - \lambda I)v = 0.
$$
Intuitively, applying $A$ to $v$ only stretches (or flips) $v$ by the factor $\lambda$, without rotating it.
:::

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Characteristic Polynomial"}
The eigenvalues of $A$ are the roots of the **characteristic polynomial**:
$$
p_A(\lambda) = \det(\lambda I - A) = \lambda^n + c_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1} + \cdots + c_0.
$$
Equivalently, $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue if and only if $\det(\lambda I - A) = 0$.
:::

Two multiplicities matter:

- **Algebraic multiplicity:** the number of times $\lambda$ appears as a root of $p_A(\lambda)$.
- **Geometric multiplicity:** $\dim\,\operatorname{Null}(A - \lambda I)$, i.e. the number of linearly independent eigenvectors for $\lambda$.

The geometric multiplicity is always $\leq$ the algebraic multiplicity. When they are equal for every eigenvalue, $A$ is called **diagonalisable**.

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Diagonalisation"}
$A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is **diagonalisable** if and only if its eigenvectors $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ form a basis of $\mathbb{C}^n$. Letting $T = [v_1 \mid \cdots \mid v_n]$:
$$
A = T \Lambda T^{-1}, \quad \Lambda = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n).
$$
All matrices with **distinct eigenvalues** are diagonalisable.
:::

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Eigenvalues and Diagonalisation of a 2x2 Matrix"}
Consider
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}3 & 1\\0 & 2\end{pmatrix}.
$$
The characteristic polynomial is $\det(\lambda I - A) = (\lambda - 3)(\lambda - 2)$, giving eigenvalues $\lambda_1 = 3$ and $\lambda_2 = 2$.

The corresponding eigenvectors are found from $(A - \lambda_i I)v_i = 0$:
$$
v_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}, \quad v_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Since the eigenvalues are distinct, $A$ is diagonalisable. Setting $T = [v_1 \mid v_2]$:
$$
T = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\0 & -1\end{pmatrix}, \quad
T^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\0 & -1\end{pmatrix}.
$$
We can verify $A = T\Lambda T^{-1}$ directly:
$$
T\Lambda T^{-1}
= \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\0 & -1\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}3 & 0\\0 & 2\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\0 & -1\end{pmatrix}
= \begin{pmatrix}3 & 1\\0 & 2\end{pmatrix} = A. \checkmark
$$
:::

## Jordan Normal Form

When $A$ is **not** diagonalisable (geometric multiplicity $<$ algebraic multiplicity for some eigenvalue), it cannot be brought to a diagonal form. The most structured form still achievable via a similarity transformation is the **Jordan normal form**. Intuitively, Jordan blocks encode the "chains" of generalised eigenvectors that arise when the eigenvectors alone do not span the full space.

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Jordan Decomposition"}
For any $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ there exists an invertible $V \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ such that
$$
A = V J V^{-1}, \qquad
J = \operatorname{blockdiag}(J_1, J_2, \ldots, J_k),
$$
where each **Jordan block** is
$$
J_i = \begin{pmatrix}\lambda_i & 1 & & \\ & \lambda_i & \ddots & \\ & & \ddots & 1 \\ & & & \lambda_i\end{pmatrix} \in \mathbb{C}^{n_i \times n_i}.
$$
If all Jordan blocks are $1 \times 1$, then $J = \Lambda$ and $A$ is diagonalisable.
:::

### Matrix Exponential via Jordan Form

The Jordan form gives a systematic way to compute $e^{At}$, the state-transition matrix of $\dot{x} = Ax$. The matrix exponential is defined via the power series
$$
e^{At} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(At)^k}{k!} = I + At + \frac{A^2 t^2}{2!} + \cdots
$$
Substituting $A = VJV^{-1}$, the key observation is that powers of $A$ simplify nicely:
$$
A^k = (VJV^{-1})^k = VJ\underbrace{V^{-1}V}_{=\,I}J\underbrace{V^{-1}V}_{=\,I}\cdots JV^{-1} = VJ^k V^{-1}.
$$
The $V^{-1}V$ pairs in the middle all collapse to the identity, so every power of $A$ carries the same outer factors $V$ and $V^{-1}$. Inserting this into the power series:
$$
e^{At} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(VJV^{-1})^k t^k}{k!}
= \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{V J^k V^{-1} t^k}{k!}
= V \left(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{J^k t^k}{k!}\right) V^{-1}
= V e^{Jt} V^{-1}.
$$
Because $J$ is block-diagonal, its exponential is also block-diagonal:
$$
e^{Jt} = \operatorname{blockdiag}(e^{J_1 t}, \ldots, e^{J_k t}).
$$
For a single Jordan block $J_i$ of size $n_i$ with eigenvalue $\lambda_i$, write $J_i = \lambda_i I + N$ where $N$ is the nilpotent shift matrix ($N^{n_i} = 0$). Since $\lambda_i I$ and $N$ commute:
$$
e^{J_i t} = e^{\lambda_i t} e^{Nt} = e^{\lambda_i t} \sum_{k=0}^{n_i - 1} \frac{t^k}{k!} N^k
= e^{\lambda_i t}
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & t & \tfrac{t^2}{2!} & \cdots & \tfrac{t^{n_i-1}}{(n_i-1)!} \\
0 & 1 & t & \cdots & \tfrac{t^{n_i-2}}{(n_i-2)!} \\
\vdots & & \ddots & & \vdots \\
0 & \cdots & 0 & 1 & t \\
0 & \cdots & & 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$
For a diagonalisable matrix (all blocks $1 \times 1$), this reduces to the familiar $e^{At} = T \operatorname{diag}(e^{\lambda_i t}) T^{-1}$.

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Matrix Exponential of a Jordan Block"}
Consider the non-diagonalisable matrix
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}\lambda & 1\\0 & \lambda\end{pmatrix}.
$$
This is already in Jordan form, so $V = I$ and $J = A$. Decomposing $J = \lambda I + N$ with $N = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}$ and noting $N^2 = 0$:
$$
e^{At} = I \cdot e^{Jt} \cdot I^{-1} = e^{\lambda t}\begin{pmatrix}1 & t\\0 & 1\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Verification by differentiation: $\tfrac{d}{dt}e^{At} = A e^{At}$:
$$
\frac{d}{dt}\left[e^{\lambda t}\begin{pmatrix}1 & t\\0 & 1\end{pmatrix}\right]
= e^{\lambda t}\begin{pmatrix}\lambda & 1 + \lambda t\\0 & \lambda\end{pmatrix}
= \begin{pmatrix}\lambda & 1\\0 & \lambda\end{pmatrix} e^{\lambda t}\begin{pmatrix}1 & t\\0 & 1\end{pmatrix} = A\,e^{At}. \checkmark
$$
The polynomial factor $t$ grows without bound as $t \to \infty$ when $\lambda = 0$, illustrating the subtlety of Jordan blocks compared to the purely exponential behaviour of diagonal matrices.
:::

## Inner Products and Orthogonality

Norms measure size, but they do not capture the notion of **angle** between vectors. Inner products provide both, and they are what turns a vector space into a proper geometric space. In control, inner products underpin the QR factorization, the Kalman filter, $H_2$ norms, and least-squares estimation.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Inner Product"}
An **inner product** on a real vector space $V$ is a map $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle : V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying, for all $u, v, w \in V$ and $a \in \mathbb{R}$:

1. *Symmetry:* $\langle u, v \rangle = \langle v, u \rangle$
2. *Linearity in first argument:* $\langle au + w, v \rangle = a\langle u, v \rangle + \langle w, v \rangle$
3. *Positive definiteness:* $\langle v, v \rangle \geq 0$, with equality only if $v = \mathbf{0}$

Every inner product induces a norm via $\|v\| = \sqrt{\langle v, v \rangle}$.
:::

The standard inner product on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is the **dot product**:
$$
\langle x, y \rangle = x^T y = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i.
$$

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Orthogonality"}
Two vectors $u, v \in V$ are **orthogonal**, written $u \perp v$, if $\langle u, v \rangle = 0$.

A set $\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$ is:

- **Orthogonal** if $\langle v_i, v_j \rangle = 0$ for all $i \neq j$
- **Orthonormal** if additionally $\langle v_i, v_i \rangle = 1$ for all $i$

An orthonormal basis is maximally convenient: coordinates are computed by dot products, $x = \sum_i \langle x, v_i \rangle v_i$, and there is no matrix inversion needed.
:::

### Orthogonal Projection

Orthogonal projection is the mathematical operation behind least-squares, observers, and the Gram-Schmidt process. The intuition is simple: given a subspace $W$, the projection of $v$ onto $W$ is the point in $W$ closest to $v$.

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Orthogonal Projection"}
Let $W \subseteq V$ be a subspace with orthonormal basis $\{q_1, \ldots, q_k\}$. The **orthogonal projection** of $v \in V$ onto $W$ is
$$
\operatorname{proj}_W(v) = \sum_{i=1}^k \langle v, q_i \rangle\, q_i = Q Q^T v,
$$
where $Q = [q_1 \mid \cdots \mid q_k]$.

The projection matrix $P_W = QQ^T$ satisfies $P_W^2 = P_W$ (idempotent) and $P_W^T = P_W$ (symmetric). The residual $v - P_W v$ is orthogonal to every vector in $W$.
:::

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Least-Squares as Projection"}
The least-squares problem $\min_x \|Ax - b\|_2$ asks: among all vectors in the column space of $A$, which one is closest to $b$?

The answer is the orthogonal projection of $b$ onto $\operatorname{Range}(A)$:
$$
\hat{b} = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T b = P_A\, b, \qquad x^* = (A^T A)^{-1} A^T b.
$$
The residual $b - \hat{b}$ is orthogonal to every column of $A$, which is exactly the normal equation $A^T(b - Ax^*) = 0$.
:::

### Gram-Schmidt Orthogonalization

Given any basis $\{a_1, \ldots, a_k\}$ of a subspace, the **Gram-Schmidt process** constructs an orthonormal basis $\{q_1, \ldots, q_k\}$ spanning the same space:
$$
\tilde{q}_i = a_i - \sum_{j=1}^{i-1} \langle a_i, q_j \rangle\, q_j, \qquad q_i = \frac{\tilde{q}_i}{\|\tilde{q}_i\|}.
$$
At each step, we subtract the components already explained by the previous basis vectors. This process is exactly what the QR factorization computes in a numerically stable way (see Chapter 1.2).

## Symmetric Matrices and Positive Definiteness

Symmetric matrices are ubiquitous in control: covariance matrices, Lyapunov matrices, Gramians, and Hessians are all symmetric. They enjoy far stronger structural properties than general matrices.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Symmetric and Hermitian Matrices"}
A matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is **symmetric** if $A = A^T$. The set of $n \times n$ symmetric matrices is denoted $\mathbb{S}^n$.

For complex matrices, the analogous condition is $A = A^H$ (conjugate transpose), called **Hermitian**.
:::

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Spectral Theorem for Symmetric Matrices"}
If $A \in \mathbb{S}^n$, then:

1. All eigenvalues of $A$ are **real**.
2. Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are **orthogonal**.
3. $A$ is **orthogonally diagonalisable**: there exists an orthogonal matrix $Q$ (i.e. $Q^T Q = I$) such that
$$
A = Q \Lambda Q^T = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i q_i q_i^T,
$$
where $\Lambda = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$ and the columns $q_i$ of $Q$ are orthonormal eigenvectors.
:::

This is a much stronger statement than general diagonalisation: the change-of-basis matrix $Q$ is not just invertible but **orthogonal**, meaning it is a rotation (or reflection). Geometrically, symmetric matrices always have a canonical set of perpendicular principal axes.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Positive Definiteness"}
A symmetric matrix $A \in \mathbb{S}^n$ is:

| Name | Notation | Condition |
|---|---|---|
| Positive definite | $A \succ 0$ | $x^T A x > 0$ for all $x \neq 0$ |
| Positive semidefinite | $A \succeq 0$ | $x^T A x \geq 0$ for all $x$ |
| Negative definite | $A \prec 0$ | $x^T A x < 0$ for all $x \neq 0$ |
| Indefinite | | $x^T A x$ takes both signs |

Equivalently (via the spectral theorem):

- $A \succ 0 \iff$ all eigenvalues $\lambda_i > 0$
- $A \succeq 0 \iff$ all eigenvalues $\lambda_i \geq 0$
:::

*Intuition:* Think of $x^T A x$ as a generalised squared length. Positive definiteness means this quantity is always positive — $A$ acts like a "distorted" dot product that never collapses any direction to zero.

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Positive Definiteness via Eigenvalues"}
Let
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}4 & 1\\1 & 3\end{pmatrix}.
$$
The eigenvalues are the roots of $\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 11 = 0$, giving $\lambda_{1,2} = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 4.62,\, 2.38$. Both are positive, so $A \succ 0$.

Alternatively, the **Sylvester criterion** checks leading principal minors: $4 > 0$ and $\det(A) = 12 - 1 = 11 > 0$, confirming $A \succ 0$.
:::

### Why Positive Definiteness Matters in Control

Positive definiteness is the algebraic analog of "everything is bounded and well-behaved." Specifically:

- **Lyapunov stability** (Ch. 12): A system $\dot{x} = Ax$ is stable if there exists $P \succ 0$ such that $A^T P + PA \prec 0$. The Lyapunov function is $V(x) = x^T P x > 0$, and its derivative $\dot{V} = x^T(A^T P + PA)x < 0$ guarantees decrease.
- **Covariance matrices** (Ch. 10): Error covariance matrices in Kalman filtering are always $\succeq 0$; losing positive semidefiniteness numerically is a common failure mode.
- **Cost functions** (Ch. 14, 15): Quadratic costs $x^T Q x + u^T R u$ in MPC require $Q \succeq 0$ and $R \succ 0$ for the problem to be well-posed.

## Trace and Determinant

The **trace** and **determinant** are two scalar summaries of a matrix that appear repeatedly in spectral analysis, stability theory, and performance metrics.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Trace and Determinant"}
For $A \in \mathbb{F}^{n \times n}$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$:

$$
\operatorname{tr}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n A_{ii} = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i
$$

$$
\det(A) = \prod_{i=1}^n \lambda_i
$$

The trace equals the sum of diagonal entries (and the sum of eigenvalues); the determinant equals the product of eigenvalues.
:::

Key properties used throughout the course:

| Property | Formula |
|---|---|
| Linearity of trace | $\operatorname{tr}(A + B) = \operatorname{tr}(A) + \operatorname{tr}(B)$ |
| Cyclic permutation | $\operatorname{tr}(ABC) = \operatorname{tr}(BCA) = \operatorname{tr}(CAB)$ |
| Similarity invariance | $\operatorname{tr}(Q^{-1}AQ) = \operatorname{tr}(A)$ |
| Determinant of product | $\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$ |
| Determinant of inverse | $\det(A^{-1}) = 1/\det(A)$ |
| $A$ singular $\iff$ | $\det(A) = 0$ |

*The cyclic permutation property* $\operatorname{tr}(ABC) = \operatorname{tr}(BCA)$ is particularly useful: the $H_2$ norm of a system can be written as $\|G\|_2^2 = \operatorname{tr}(B^T P B)$ where $P$ is a Lyapunov solution, and cyclic permutation allows algebraic simplification of such expressions.

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Trace and $H_2$ Performance"}
For a stable LTI system with state-space matrices $(A, B, C)$, the $H_2$ norm squared satisfies
$$
\|G\|_2^2 = \operatorname{tr}(C W_c C^T),
$$
where $W_c$ is the **controllability Gramian** (see Ch. 11), the solution to $AW_c + W_cA^T + BB^T = 0$.
The trace here sums the output energy contributions from each state direction — a direct link between the algebraic concept and system performance.
:::

## Norms

Vector spaces carry purely algebraic structure. To speak about *size*, *distance*, and *convergence*, we equip them with a **norm**. In control systems, norms appear in stability margins, performance specifications, and $H_\infty$ design.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Vector Norm"}
A **norm** on a vector space $(V, \mathbb{F})$ is a function $\|\cdot\|: V \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying, for all $v, w \in V$ and $a \in \mathbb{F}$:

1. *Triangle inequality:* $\|v + w\| \leq \|v\| + \|w\|$
2. *Absolute homogeneity:* $\|av\| = |a|\,\|v\|$
3. *Positive definiteness:* $\|v\| = 0 \Leftrightarrow v = \mathbf{0}$
:::

For $x \in \mathbb{F}^n$, the most common norms are:

| Name | Formula | Intuition |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 1-norm | $\|x\|_1 = \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|$ | City-block distance; promotes sparsity |
| Euclidean (2-norm) | $\|x\|_2 = \left(\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|^2\right)^{1/2}$ | Standard geometric length |
| $\infty$-norm | $\|x\|_\infty = \max_{i} |x_i|$ | Worst-case component |

All norms on a finite-dimensional space are **equivalent**: for any two norms $\|\cdot\|_a$ and $\|\cdot\|_b$ there exist $m_l, m_u > 0$ such that
$$
m_l \|x\|_a \leq \|x\|_b \leq m_u \|x\|_a \quad \forall\, x \in V.
$$
This means convergence and stability conclusions do not depend on the specific choice of norm in finite dimensions.

## Matrix Norms

Matrix norms measure how much a linear map can amplify vectors. There are two important families.

### Induced (Operator) Norms

The **induced norm** of $A$ with respect to a vector norm $\|\cdot\|$ is
$$
\|A\| = \sup_{x \neq 0} \frac{\|Ax\|}{\|x\|} = \sup_{\|x\|=1} \|Ax\|.
$$
It answers the question: *what is the worst-case amplification factor of $A$?*

For the Euclidean vector norm, the induced matrix norm is the **spectral norm**:
$$
\|A\|_2 = \sigma_{\max}(A),
$$
the largest singular value of $A$ (see Ch. 1.2). This is the most commonly used matrix norm in control because it directly measures the largest gain of the system in any direction.

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Spectral Norm and Frobenius Norm"}
For $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ with singular values $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_{\min(m,n)} \geq 0$:

$$
\|A\|_2 = \sigma_1 \qquad \text{(spectral norm, induced by Euclidean vector norm)}
$$

$$
\|A\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i,j} A_{ij}^2} = \sqrt{\operatorname{tr}(A^T A)} = \sqrt{\sum_i \sigma_i^2} \qquad \text{(Frobenius norm)}
$$

The Frobenius norm is not an induced norm, but it is often more convenient computationally because it is defined entry-wise.
:::

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Spectral vs. Frobenius Norm"}
Let
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}3 & 0\\0 & 1\end{pmatrix}.
$$
The singular values are $\sigma_1 = 3$, $\sigma_2 = 1$ (since $A$ is diagonal and positive).

$$
\|A\|_2 = 3, \qquad \|A\|_F = \sqrt{9 + 1} = \sqrt{10} \approx 3.16.
$$

The spectral norm captures the worst-case direction (amplification by 3 along the first axis); the Frobenius norm is a root-mean-square measure across all directions.
:::

### Key Inequalities

These inequalities appear when bounding system responses and perturbation effects:

$$
\|A\|_2 \leq \|A\|_F \leq \sqrt{n}\,\|A\|_2
$$
$$
\|AB\|_2 \leq \|A\|_2 \|B\|_2 \qquad \text{(submultiplicativity)}
$$
$$
\|Ax\|_2 \leq \|A\|_2 \|x\|_2
$$

The condition number $\kappa(A) = \|A\|_2 \|A^{-1}\|_2 = \sigma_1 / \sigma_n$ quantifies how sensitive the solution of $Ax = b$ is to perturbations in $b$ or $A$.

## Why This Matters for Control

The concepts developed in this chapter are not ends in themselves. They directly enable the core tools of linear control:

- **Eigenvalues** of the system matrix $A$ determine stability (Ch. 4)
- **Jordan form** and matrix exponential govern the time evolution $e^{At}$ of state-space systems (Ch. 3)
- **Rank conditions** on matrices built from $A$ and $B$ determine controllability (Ch. 8)
- **Inner products and orthogonality** underlie the QR factorization (Ch. 1.2) and the Kalman filter gain derivation (Ch. 10)
- **Symmetric matrices and positive definiteness** are the language of Lyapunov stability (Ch. 12) and LMI-based synthesis (Ch. 17)
- **Trace** connects Gramians to $H_2$ performance metrics (Ch. 11)
- **Spectral norm** $\|A\|_2 = \sigma_{\max}$ is the building block of the $H_\infty$ system norm (Ch. 20)
- **Norms** provide the metric foundation for convergence, stability margins, and robust design

## Exercises

::: {.callout-warning title="Exercise"}
**1.** Let
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1\\0 & 3\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Compute the eigenvalues and eigenvectors. Is $A$ diagonalisable? If yes, write $A = T\Lambda T^{-1}$ and verify the factorisation numerically.

**2.** Show that similar matrices have the same characteristic polynomial, and hence the same eigenvalues.

**3.** Let
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\0 & 1\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Compute $e^{At}$ using the Jordan form. Identify the polynomial-in-$t$ factor and explain what it implies for the growth of $\|e^{At}\|$ as $t \to \infty$.

**4.** Let $\{a_1, a_2\} = \{(1, 1, 0)^T, (1, 0, 1)^T\}$. Apply Gram-Schmidt to obtain an orthonormal basis $\{q_1, q_2\}$ for $\operatorname{Span}\{a_1, a_2\}$.

**5.** For the matrix
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}5 & 2\\2 & 3\end{pmatrix},
$$
verify the spectral theorem: find eigenvalues and orthonormal eigenvectors $q_1, q_2$ and confirm $A = \lambda_1 q_1 q_1^T + \lambda_2 q_2 q_2^T$.

**6.** Compute $\|A\|_2$ and $\|A\|_F$ for $A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 2\\0 & 3\end{pmatrix}$ and verify $\|A\|_2 \leq \|A\|_F$.
:::

::: {.callout-caution collapse="true" title="Solution — Show solution"}
**1.** Characteristic polynomial:
$$
\det(\lambda I - A) = (\lambda-2)(\lambda-3) = 0 \implies \lambda_1 = 2,\; \lambda_2 = 3.
$$
Distinct eigenvalues $\Rightarrow$ $A$ is diagonalisable. Eigenvectors:
$$
\lambda_1 = 2:\; v_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad
\lambda_2 = 3:\; v_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Set $T = [v_1 \mid v_2] = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\0 & 1\end{pmatrix}$ and $T^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & -1\\0 & 1\end{pmatrix}$. Verify:
$$
T\Lambda T^{-1}
= \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\0 & 1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2 & 0\\0 & 3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1 & -1\\0 & 1\end{pmatrix}
= \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1\\0 & 3\end{pmatrix} = A. \checkmark
$$

**2.** Let $\tilde{A} = Q^{-1}AQ$. Then:
$$
\det(\lambda I - \tilde{A})
= \det\!\left(Q^{-1}(\lambda I - A)Q\right)
= \det(Q^{-1})\cdot\det(\lambda I - A)\cdot\det(Q) = \det(\lambda I - A),
$$
since $\det(Q^{-1})\det(Q) = \det(I) = 1$.

**3.** $A = I + N$ with $N = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\0 & 0\end{pmatrix}$, $N^2 = 0$. The eigenvalue is $\lambda = 1$ with a $2\times 2$ Jordan block:
$$
e^{At} = e^{t}\begin{pmatrix}1 & t\\0 & 1\end{pmatrix}.
$$
The factor $t$ grows polynomially. The Jordan block produces growth $\|e^{At}\| \sim t\,e^{t}$, faster than a simple eigenvalue at $1$ alone would suggest.

**4.** Step 1: $q_1 = a_1 / \|a_1\| = (1,1,0)^T/\sqrt{2}$.
Step 2: $\tilde{q}_2 = a_2 - \langle a_2, q_1\rangle q_1 = (1,0,1)^T - \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cdot\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1,0)^T = (1,0,1)^T - \tfrac{1}{2}(1,1,0)^T = (\tfrac{1}{2}, -\tfrac{1}{2}, 1)^T$.
Normalise: $q_2 = \tilde{q}_2/\|\tilde{q}_2\| = (\tfrac{1}{2}, -\tfrac{1}{2}, 1)^T / \sqrt{3/2} = (1,-1,2)^T/\sqrt{6}$.

**5.** Eigenvalues from $\lambda^2 - 8\lambda + 11 = 0$: $\lambda_{1,2} = 4 \pm \sqrt{5}$.
Eigenvectors (normalised) are $q_1 \propto (2, \sqrt{5}-1)^T$ and $q_2 \propto (2, -\sqrt{5}-1)^T$ (orthogonal by the spectral theorem since $A$ is symmetric). The decomposition $A = \lambda_1 q_1 q_1^T + \lambda_2 q_2 q_2^T$ can be verified by direct multiplication.

**6.** Singular values of $A$: compute $A^T A = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\2&3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\0&3\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1&2\\2&13\end{pmatrix}$.
Eigenvalues of $A^TA$: $\lambda^2 - 14\lambda + 9 = 0$, giving $\lambda_{1,2} = 7 \pm 2\sqrt{10}$.
So $\|A\|_2 = \sqrt{7 + 2\sqrt{10}} \approx 3.22$ and $\|A\|_F = \sqrt{1+4+0+9} = \sqrt{14} \approx 3.74$.
Indeed $\|A\|_2 \approx 3.22 \leq 3.74 \approx \|A\|_F$. $\checkmark$
:::
