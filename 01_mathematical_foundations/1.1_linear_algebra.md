# 1.1 Linear Algebra

Linear algebra is the mathematical backbone of control systems theory.
Almost every concept — state-space models, stability analysis, controllability, observability — reduces to operations on vectors and matrices.
This chapter reviews the essential structures: vector spaces, linear maps, matrix properties, and eigenvalues.

---

## Fields and Vector Spaces

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Field"}
A **field** \(\mathbb{F}, +, \cdot\) is a commutative ring in which every non-zero element has a multiplicative inverse.
The most important examples for control are \(\mathbb{R}\) and \(\mathbb{C}\).
:::

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Vector Space (Linear Space)"}
A **vector space** \(V, \mathbb{F}\) is a set \(V\) of vectors together with a field \(\mathbb{F}\) of scalars, equipped with:

- **Vector addition** \(+: V \times V \to V\) that is associative, commutative, has an identity \(\mathbf{0}\), and every element has an additive inverse.
- **Scalar multiplication** \(\cdot: \mathbb{F} \times V \to V\) that is associative, distributive over both vector and scalar addition, and satisfies \(1 \cdot v = v\).
:::

The most relevant instance for control is \(\mathbb{R}^n\) over \(\mathbb{R}\), which serves as the state, input, and output space of linear systems.

---

## Subspaces, Span, and Basis

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Subspace"}
A non-empty subset \(W \subseteq V\) is a **subspace** of \(V, \mathbb{F}\) if it is closed under linear combinations:
\[
a_1 w_1 + a_2 w_2 \in W \quad \forall\, w_1, w_2 \in W,\; a_1, a_2 \in \mathbb{F}.
\]
:::

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Span and Linear Independence"}
The **span** of a set \(S \subseteq V\) is
\[
\operatorname{Span}(S) = \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i v_i \;\Big|\; n \in \mathbb{N},\, a_i \in \mathbb{F},\, v_i \in S \right\}.
\]
A set \(S\) is **linearly independent** if the only solution to \(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i v_i = \mathbf{0}\) is \(a_i = 0\) for all \(i\).
:::

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Basis and Dimension"}
A set \(\{b_1, \ldots, b_n\}\) is a **basis** of \(V\) if it is linearly independent and \(\operatorname{Span}\{b_1,\ldots,b_n\} = V\).
If such a finite basis exists, \(n\) is the **dimension** of \(V\), written \(\dim V = n\).
Every basis of a finite-dimensional space has the same number of elements.
:::

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Canonical Basis of \(\mathbb{R}^3\)"}
The standard basis \(\{e_1, e_2, e_3\} = \{(1,0,0),(0,1,0),(0,0,1)\}\) is a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^3\) and \(\dim \mathbb{R}^3 = 3\).
Any three linearly independent vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) also form a valid basis.
:::

## Linear Maps and Matrix Representation

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Linear Map"}
A function \(A: U \to V\) between two vector spaces over the same field \(\mathbb{F}\) is **linear** if
\[
A(a_1 u_1 + a_2 u_2) = a_1 A(u_1) + a_2 A(u_2) \quad \forall\, u_1, u_2 \in U,\; a_1, a_2 \in \mathbb{F}.
\]
:::

Once bases are fixed for \(U\) (dimension \(n\)) and \(V\) (dimension \(m\)), every linear map \(A: U \to V\) has a unique **matrix representation** \(A \in \mathbb{F}^{m \times n}\) such that the action of \(A\) corresponds to matrix–vector multiplication.

Two important associated subspaces are:

- **Null space**: \(\operatorname{Null}(A) = \{u \in U \mid Au = \mathbf{0}\}\), a subspace of \(U\).
- **Range space**: \(\operatorname{Range}(A) = \{Au \mid u \in U\}\), a subspace of \(V\).

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Rank–Nullity"}
For any matrix \(A \in \mathbb{F}^{m \times n}\):
\[
\operatorname{rank}(A) + \operatorname{nullity}(A) = n,
\]
where \(\operatorname{rank}(A) = \dim\operatorname{Range}(A)\) and \(\operatorname{nullity}(A) = \dim\operatorname{Null}(A)\).

Additionally, \(0 \le \operatorname{rank}(A) \le \min(m, n)\).
:::

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Invertibility of Square Matrices"}
For \(A \in \mathbb{F}^{n \times n}\), the following are equivalent:

1. \(A\) is invertible (\(\det A \neq 0\)).
2. \(A\) is bijective as a linear map.
3. \(\operatorname{rank}(A) = n\).
4. \(\operatorname{nullity}(A) = 0\).
5. The columns (and rows) of \(A\) are linearly independent.
6. None of the eigenvalues of \(A\) equals zero.
:::

## Change of Basis

Given two bases for the same space, representations of vectors and linear maps change according to a **similarity transformation**.

If \(A \in \mathbb{F}^{n \times n}\) is the representation of a linear map with respect to one basis and \(Q \in \mathbb{F}^{n \times n}\) is the invertible matrix mapping between the two bases, then the representation with respect to the new basis is:
\[
\tilde{A} = Q^{-1} A Q.
\]
Matrices related this way are called **similar** and share the same eigenvalues (spectrum).

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Change of Basis in \(\mathbb{R}^2\)"}
Let \(A\) be represented in the canonical basis \(\{e_1, e_2\}\) and let \(Q = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}\) be the change-of-basis matrix to a new basis \(\{e_1, e_1+e_2\}\).
The new representation is \(\tilde{A} = Q^{-1}AQ\).
:::

## Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

::: {.callout-note title="Definition — Eigenvalue and Eigenvector"}
A scalar \(\lambda \in \mathbb{C}\) is an **eigenvalue** of \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) if there exists a non-zero vector \(v \in \mathbb{C}^n\) (the **eigenvector**) such that
\[
Av = \lambda v \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad (A - \lambda I)v = 0.
\]
:::

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Characteristic Polynomial"}
The eigenvalues of \(A\) are the roots of the **characteristic polynomial**:
\[
p_A(\lambda) = \det(\lambda I - A) = \lambda^n + c_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1} + \cdots + c_0.
\]
Equivalently, \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue if and only if \(\det(\lambda I - A) = 0\).
:::

Two multiplicities matter:

- **Algebraic multiplicity**: number of times \(\lambda\) appears as a root of \(p_A(\lambda)\).
- **Geometric multiplicity**: \(\dim\operatorname{Null}(A - \lambda I)\), i.e., the number of linearly independent eigenvectors for \(\lambda\).

The geometric multiplicity is always \(\leq\) the algebraic multiplicity. When they are equal for every eigenvalue, \(A\) is called **semi-simple** (or diagonalizable).

::: {.callout-important title="Theorem — Diagonalization"}
\(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is **semi-simple** if and only if its eigenvectors \(\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}\) form a basis of \(\mathbb{C}^n\). In that case, letting \(T = [v_1 \mid \cdots \mid v_n]\):
\[
A = T \Lambda T^{-1}, \quad \Lambda = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n).
\]
All **simple** matrices (with distinct eigenvalues) are semi-simple.
:::

::: {.callout-tip title="Example — Eigenvalues of a 2×2 Matrix"}
Consider \(A = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\-1 & 0\end{pmatrix}\).
The characteristic polynomial is \(\det(\lambda I - A) = \lambda^2 + 1\), giving eigenvalues \(\lambda_{1,2} = \pm j\).
The eigenvectors are \(v_1 = c\begin{pmatrix}1\\j\end{pmatrix}\) and \(v_2 = c\begin{pmatrix}1\\-j\end{pmatrix}\) for any \(c \neq 0\).
:::

## Why This Matters for Control

These linear algebra concepts are not just abstract — they directly enable the core tools of linear control:

- **Eigenvalues** of the system matrix \(A\) determine stability (Chapter 4).
- **Rank conditions** on matrices built from \(A\) and \(B\) determine controllability (Chapter 4, 8).
- **Change of basis / diagonalization** simplifies the computation of the matrix exponential \(e^{At}\), which is the state-transition matrix (Chapter 3, 4).
- **Null and range spaces** describe which states are reachable or observable.

::: {.callout-warning title="Exercise"}
1. Let \(A = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1\\0 & 3\end{pmatrix}\). Compute the eigenvalues and eigenvectors. Is \(A\) diagonalizable?
2. Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\) and \(B \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}\). Without computing any trajectories, state the condition under which the system \(\dot{x} = Ax + Bu\) is controllable (hint: rank condition on the controllability matrix).
3. Show that similar matrices have the same characteristic polynomial, and hence the same eigenvalues.
:::

::: {.callout-caution collapse="true" title="Solution — Show solution"}
1. Characteristic polynomial: \(\det(\lambda I - A) = (\lambda-2)(\lambda-3)\), so \(\lambda_1=2, \lambda_2=3\). Both eigenvalues are distinct, so \(A\) is semi-simple (diagonalizable). Eigenvectors: for \(\lambda_1=2\), \(v_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}\); for \(\lambda_2=3\), \(v_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}\).
2. The system is controllable if and only if \(\operatorname{rank}\begin{pmatrix}B & AB & \cdots & A^{n-1}B\end{pmatrix} = n\).
3. Let \(\tilde{A} = Q^{-1}AQ\). Then \(\det(\lambda I - \tilde{A}) = \det(\lambda I - Q^{-1}AQ) = \det(Q^{-1}(\lambda I - A)Q) = \det(Q^{-1})\det(\lambda I - A)\det(Q) = \det(\lambda I - A)\), since \(\det(Q^{-1})\det(Q) = 1\).
:::
